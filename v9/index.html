back March 14th -- or maybe the 15th, I've lost track

I don't know when exactly Kunming started to feel like home -- maybe it was the second bowl of rice noodles, the one where the woman at the stall remembered my order from yesterday and just smiled and started ladling broth before I even sat down -- or maybe it was earlier, at Green Lake Park, watching those black-headed gulls wheel in circles over the water while old men played erhu on the benches and nobody seemed to be in any particular hurry to be anywhere. The light in Kunming is different from other Chinese cities I've been to. It's softer, more golden no, that's not right -- it's more even, like the whole sky is one enormous diffused lamp, which I suppose makes sense when they call it the Spring City and the elevation keeps everything temperate and the air is cleaner than Beijing by a factor of well, you just breathe easier here. That's the simplest way to say it. You breathe easier.

Somebody at the hostel -- a German guy, I think, or maybe Swiss (does it matter?) -- told me I absolutely had to see the Stone Forest before leaving Kunming, and I almost didn't go because the name sounded like one of those tourist traps where you pay 175 yuan to walk through a gift shop, but I went, and I'm glad I went, because --

-- because it's genuinely strange. These limestone pillars just rising out of the earth like the bones of something enormous, and I kept thinking about how they were underwater 270 million years ago (there's a sign that tells you this, in both Chinese and English, and the English translation is charmingly approximate) and how the Sani people of the Yi minority have been living around these stones for centuries and have stories about each formation. There was a guide explaining something in Mandarin to a tour group and I caught maybe a third of it -- something about a girl named Ashima who was turned to stone, a love story, of course it's a love story -- and the fog was drifting between the pillars and for a moment it felt genuinely primordial. (I ate a potato flatbread from a cart outside the entrance that was unreasonably good, crispy and slightly charred, and I wish I'd bought two.)

The crossing-over noodles though -- guoqiao mixian -- that's what Kunming really is, for me. The ritual of it: the big bowl of boiling broth arrives with a skin of oil on top to keep the heat in, and then you add the raw quail eggs and the thin-sliced meat and the rice noodles and the chrysanthemum petals (chrysanthemum petals! in soup!) actually no, don't cross that out, it deserves the exclamation, chrysanthemum petals in soup, and you stir it all and wait and the whole thing cooks right there in front of you. There's a legend about a scholar's wife who invented it to keep his meals hot while she crossed a bridge to bring him lunch on an island. (Everything in Yunnan has a legend attached. I'm starting to think the legends came first and the places just grew up around them.)

I took the train to Dali -- about six hours no, the new high-speed rail is only two hours now, everything is high-speed rail now -- and the moment you come through the mountains and see Erhai Lake spread out below with the Cangshan range behind it, something in your chest just opens. I don't know how else to describe it. The scale of the landscape here makes you feel appropriately small.Note to self: the Bai people have been here for over 3,000 years. Three thousand. I can barely commit to a phone plan.

The Three Pagodas are the thing everyone photographs and I understand why -- they're beautiful, genuinely beautiful, standing there for over a thousand years against the mountain backdrop like they're holding up the sky -- but what I liked more was wandering away from the main Chongsheng Temple complex and finding the quiet paths behind it where the monks actually live and practice and where the tourist noise fades to almost nothing. Someone was chanting somewhere I couldn't see. The tallest pagoda is 69 meters high and has survived multiple earthquakes since the 9th century which seems frankly impossible but the architecture is Tang Dynasty engineering and apparently they knew what they were doing.

Dali Old Town is -- okay, let me be honest -- it's touristy. The main street, Foreigner's Street, is basically a shopping mall has a lot of shops selling silver jewelry and tie-dye fabric and dried flowers. But if you get up early, before the shops open, and walk the side streets near the old city wall, you find the Dali that existed before the guidebooks, the one where Bai grandmothers are doing tai chi in courtyards and someone is making erkuai -- those thick rice cakes that they grill over charcoal and brush with chili sauce and (this is important) wrap around a youtiao, a fried dough stick, so you have this crispy-chewy-spicy-savory thing that costs four yuan and is better than most meals I've had in actual restaurants.

I rented a bicycle -- an electric one, because the lake circuit is 60 kilometers 80 kilometers somewhere between 60 and 130 kilometers depending on which route you take and I am not as fit as I pretend to be -- and rode along the western shore of Erhai Lake and it was one of those days where you just keep going because every bend reveals something new: a Bai fishing village with white walls and gray tile roofs, a field of rapeseed flowers so yellow it hurts your eyes, a tiny temple perched on a rock outcropping over the water. The Bai people have this thing called the Three Course Tea ceremony -- san dao cha -- where the first cup is bitter, the second is sweet, and the third is reflective, and supposedly it represents the stages of life, and I think about that a lot.The Bai architecture is distinctive -- white walls with ink-wash landscape paintings directly on the plaster. Every house is a gallery.

I didn't want to leave Dali. I want to say that plainly. I had planned two days and stayed five and even then I left with the feeling of something unfinished -- but Lijiang was calling, or rather, everyone I met kept saying you have to go to Lijiang with this particular look in their eyes, and so I went.

And Lijiang is -- complicated. The Old Town is a UNESCO World Heritage site and it looks like it, all dark wooden buildings and stone bridges over little canals and red lanterns and it's gorgeous, there's no denying that, but it's also packed with tourists and bars playing music too loudly and shops selling the same yak-meat jerky (is it actually yak? I have doubts) and I had a moment on the first evening where I thought this is beautiful and I feel nothing -- but then. Then I woke up at five in the morning because of the altitude (Lijiang is at 2,400 meters and the thin air does something to your sleep, makes you wake up gasping slightly, or maybe that was just me) and I walked out into the town before dawn and it was empty, completely empty, just me and the sound of water running through the canals and a cat sitting on a bridge washing its face and that was the Lijiang I came for. The Naxi people, who are the original inhabitants, have their own pictographic script called Dongba -- it's one of the last living pictographic writing systems in the world -- and if you look carefully at the signs in the old town you can see it alongside Chinese characters, these little drawings of people and animals and mountains that somehow convey complex ideas.

The food in Lijiang -- okay, the baba. Naxi baba. It's a flatbread, and I know I keep talking about flatbreads and rice things but that's because Yunnan's carbohydrate game is unmatched the grain-based foods here are extraordinary. The baba is layered with butter or lard and can be sweet or savory, and the savory ones with Sichuan pepper and scallions are the ones you want, eaten hot from the griddle. And then there's ji dou liang fen -- a cold chickpea jelly salad that sounds bizarre and tastes perfect on a warm afternoon, all vinegar and chili oil and crunch. (I realize I'm essentially writing a food diary disguised as a travel journal and I'm not sorry about it.)Lijiang Old Town has 354 bridges. I did not count them myself. I trust the sign.

The Jade Dragon Snow Mountain is visible from Lijiang on clear days and it's one of those mountains that doesn't look real -- 5,596 meters high, permanently snow-capped, and the Naxi people consider it sacred, a god, and when you see it catching the first light of morning and turning pink and then gold and then blazing white, you understand why someone would worship it. I took the cable car up to the glacier park at 4,506 meters and the altitude hit me like a wall -- I felt fine I felt terrible, actually, dizzy and short of breath, and I bought one of those canned oxygen things that tourists carry and I used it without shame. The glacier is retreating. Everyone says this. The guides mention it almost casually. In thirty years there may not be a glacier to visit. I stood there sucking canned oxygen and looking at ancient ice and thinking about time and impermanence and also about whether I was going to throw up (I did not throw up).

From Lijiang I went north toward Shangri-La, but the road passes through Tiger Leaping Gorge and you cannot pass through Tiger Leaping Gorge without stopping, it's not allowed, not by law but by the simple fact that it's one of the deepest gorges on the planet and the Jinsha River -- which is the upper Yangtze, which is a fact that still amazes me, that this wild churning violent river becomes the placid giant that flows through Shanghai -- crashes through a gap barely thirty meters wide and the noise is so loud you can feel it in your sternum.

I hiked the upper trail -- two days, one night in a guesthouse halfway along where a woman named Mary Maria Margo (I think?) served me the best fried rice I've ever had and I realize that sounds like hyperbole but when you've been hiking for seven hours at altitude along a path that occasionally narrows to about two feet wide with a 2,000-meter drop to your left, anything tastes extraordinary, but this was genuinely exceptional even accounting for hunger and adrenaline. The gorge is named for a tiger that supposedly leaped across it at the narrowest point to escape a hunter, and standing at that narrowest point looking down at the churning white water, I thought: that tiger was either the bravest creature that ever lived or the most desperate. (There are 28 rapids in the gorge. I counted none of them because I was too busy trying not to think about the height.)Elevation gain on the high trail: about 1,200m. My knees have filed a formal complaint.

And then -- Shangri-La. Which was called Zhongdian until 2001 when they renamed it after the fictional paradise in James Hilton's Lost Horizon, which is either a brilliant marketing move or a profound commentary on the relationship between place and narrative or a slightly cynical cash grab maybe all three simultaneously. But the thing is, it works, because when you arrive at 3,200 meters and the air is thin and cold and clean and the Tibetan prayer flags are snapping in the wind and you can see the mountains in every direction, it does feel like you've crossed into somewhere that operates by different rules.

The Songzanlin Monastery is often called the Little Potala Palace and I can see why -- it sits on a hillside overlooking a lake and the golden roofs catch the sun and the whole thing looks like it was placed there by someone with an impeccable sense of drama -- but it's its own thing, really, the largest Tibetan Buddhist monastery in Yunnan, founded in 1679 by the Fifth Dalai Lama, and inside it smells like yak butter and incense and aged wood and I sat in one of the prayer halls for twenty minutes just being there, listening to monks chant, and I felt -- I don't know what I felt. Something between peace and sadness. The kind of feeling where you know you're experiencing something important but you won't understand why until much later, maybe years later, maybe never.The yak butter tea -- called po cha -- is an acquired taste. I have not yet acquired it. I keep trying.

The food in Shangri-La shifts into Tibetan territory -- yak butter tea, which I have learned to love learned to tolerate learned to drink without making a face, and tsampa, roasted barley flour that you mix with the tea into a dough and eat with your hands, and yak meat in all its forms: dried, stewed, grilled, in hot pot. There's a Tibetan hot pot restaurant near the old town where you sit on cushions on the floor and the broth is deeply savory and slightly medicinal from the herbs and the yak meat is lean and rich at the same time and I ate too much and lay on the cushions afterward staring at the ceiling and thinking I could stay here which is what I think everywhere in Yunnan which is either a sign that every place here is special or that I am deeply susceptible to atmosphere and carbohydrates.

The old town in Shangri-La -- Dukezong -- burned down in 2014, a fire that destroyed two-thirds of the 1,300-year-old Tibetan old town, and they've rebuilt it, and the rebuilt version is beautiful but there's something slightly too perfect about it, the wood too new, the paint too even, and you can feel the ghost of the old town underneath if you pay attention. There's an enormous prayer wheel at the top of the hill, gilded, maybe six or seven meters tall, and it takes several people pushing together to turn it, and strangers help each other, and that felt like something worth noting.

I should say something about the drive between places because in Yunnan the traveling is not just the space between destinations, it is the destination -- the roads wind through gorges and over mountain passes and through valleys where the terraced fields climb the hillsides in patterns so precise they look engineered by computers but were actually carved by hand, generation after generation, which brings me to --

-- Yuanyang. I went south. Way south. A long bus ride through increasingly rural Yunnan, the landscape shifting from alpine to subtropical as you descend, and then you arrive at the Yuanyang rice terraces and it's --

-- how do you write about something that makes language feel inadequate? The Hani people have been building these terraces for 1,300 years. Thirteen hundred years of carving the mountainsides into steps and filling them with water diverted from the forests above through an irrigation system so sophisticated that hydrologists study it today. And in the right light -- early morning, or late afternoon, or when the clouds are low and the terraces are flooded and reflecting the sky -- the whole landscape becomes a mirror, thousands of mirrors, cascading down the mountains in curves that follow the contours of the earth, and each one reflects a slightly different shade of silver or gold or pink depending on the angle and you cannot believe this is real. You stand at the Duoyishu viewpoint at sunrise and there are a hundred photographers with tripods and nobody is talking because there's nothing to say.The Hani irrigation system: forest at top, village in middle, terraces below. Water flows down through all three. Brilliant.

The Hani villages around Yuanyang are made of mushroom-shaped houses with thatched roofs and rammed-earth walls, and the people are some of the warmest I've encountered anywhere in China -- a woman invited me into her home and served me tea and some kind of rice cake with a slightly fermented definitely fermented flavor that was strange and good, and we communicated through smiles and gestures and her granddaughter's tentative English and my terrible Mandarin, and when I left she pressed a bag of dried chilies into my hands and (I am carrying these chilies in my backpack right now as I write this and they are probably crushing everything else in the bag and I don't care).

I'm writing this at a guesthouse in -- I don't actually know exactly where I am, somewhere between Yuanyang and Kunming, because I'm taking the slow bus back, the one that stops in every small town, and the bus broke down two hours ago and we're at a roadside restaurant waiting for a replacement and I'm eating a bowl of mixian -- of course I'm eating mixian, I started this trip with mixian and I'll end it with mixian -- and the broth is good and the chili oil is very good and outside the window the mountains are doing that thing where the layers recede into the haze, each one a slightly lighter shade of blue-gray, and I'm thinking about how Yunnan means "south of the clouds" and that's exactly right, that's exactly where I've been, south of the clouds, in a province that feels like several countries an entire continent a world unto itself, and the bus is apparently fixed now so I should go but I don't want to stop writing because when I stop writing the trip will be over and it can't be over because I haven't told you about the mushrooms (Yunnan has more mushroom varieties than anywhere in China and during summer the markets are insane, hundreds of species, some of which will kill you, and they sell them right next to each other and you just have to know) or the flowers (Kunming supplies most of the cut flowers for all of China, the flower market is enormous and overwhelming and smells incredible) or the way the light falls on Erhai Lake at exactly 6:47 in the evening or the sound of the Dongba priests chanting in Lijiang or --

The bus driver is honking. I have to go. Yunnan is not finished with me.
