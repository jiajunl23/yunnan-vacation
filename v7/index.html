My Yunnan Storybook

A hand-drawn journey through China's most magical province

Come along with me on a winding road through ancient towns, misty mountains, and terraced hillsides. I kept a little journal of the whole trip, and here it is -- pictures, doodles, and all.

1

Kunming and the Stone Forest

Our adventure begins in the Spring City, where the weather is so perfect year-round that the locals seem genuinely confused by the concept of winter coats. Kunming welcomed us with wide boulevards, flower markets overflowing with blooms I could not name, and the pleasant shock of a Chinese mega-city that actually feels relaxed.

The Stone Forest -- like walking through a giant's chess set

An hour outside the city, we arrived at the Stone Forest -- Shilin. Imagine a field of towering limestone pillars, some thin as needles, some wide as buses, all packed together like a crowd frozen in stone. I spent a happy afternoon getting wonderfully lost in its narrow pathways, the gray rock cool under my fingertips.

Kunming is the kind of place you plan for two days and wish you had given a week. The noodles alone deserve their own chapter, but I will save that for the food section below.

"Kunming sits at 1,900 meters above sea level. That is why the weather stays so mild -- eternal spring at altitude!"

2

Dali

Next stop: a town sandwiched between a mountain and a lake, which sounds like something from a fairy tale and honestly kind of is. Dali sits on the western shore of Erhai Lake with the Cangshan mountain range rising dramatically behind it, like a painted backdrop someone forgot to take down after the play.

The Three Pagodas -- standing guard for over a thousand years

The Three Pagodas are Dali's most famous residents, and they have been here since the 9th century. The tallest one reaches 70 meters, and I craned my neck so far back trying to see the top that I nearly fell over. The reflection pool in front catches them perfectly on still mornings.

Erhai Lake -- the ear-shaped sea

We rented bikes and rode the lakeshore road, which was exactly as idyllic as it sounds. Little Bai-style villages dot the route, white-walled houses with upturned eaves and hand-painted floral details on every surface. Dali moves at a different pace than the rest of China -- slower, dreamier, with more tie-dye.

"Erhai means 'ear-shaped sea.' The Bai people who live here have been fishing these waters for thousands of years, sometimes using trained cormorants."

3

Lijiang

We wandered into a maze of canals and dark-tiled roofs, and for a moment I genuinely was not sure what century it was. Lijiang Old Town is a UNESCO World Heritage Site that has been lived in continuously for over 800 years, and it wears that history lightly -- crookedly, charmingly, like a well-loved hat.

Lijiang Old Town -- 800 years of cobblestones

The Naxi people built this town without a single straight road, and I am fairly certain that was a deliberate artistic choice rather than poor city planning. Every turn reveals a new canal, a new courtyard, a new grandmother sitting in a doorway peeling something. The water channels that wind through the streets were once the town's only water supply.

Jade Dragon Snow Mountain -- always watching over Lijiang

Looming over town is Jade Dragon Snow Mountain, its peak at 5,596 meters perpetually draped in snow. On clear mornings, you can see it from the rooftops of the old town, and it stops you in your tracks every single time. We took a cable car partway up and stepped out into a world of thin air and blinding white.

"The Naxi people developed their own pictographic script called Dongba -- one of the last living pictographic writing systems on Earth. You will see it carved everywhere in Lijiang."

4

Tiger Leaping Gorge

This is where things get wild -- literally. Tiger Leaping Gorge is one of the deepest gorges in the world, carved by the Jinsha River as it roars between Jade Dragon Snow Mountain and Haba Snow Mountain. Standing at the rim looking down, the river looks like a thin white ribbon. Then you realize that "thin ribbon" is actually a violent, thundering torrent of whitewater, and the scale of everything recalibrates in your brain.

Tiger Leaping Gorge -- 3,900 meters from river to peak

We hiked the high trail over two days. The path hugs the mountainside, sometimes wide and easy, sometimes a narrow ledge that made my palms sweat. Guest houses along the route are run by local families who will feed you enormous plates of fried rice and vegetables and charge you almost nothing. The views are so dramatic they feel unearned.

The gorge gets its name from a legend about a tiger that escaped hunters by leaping across the river at its narrowest point, using a rock in the middle as a stepping stone. I looked at the gap and thought: that must have been a very motivated tiger.

"The gorge is about 15 kilometers long and up to 3,900 meters deep. For reference, the Grand Canyon maxes out at about 1,800 meters. This place is not messing around."

5

Shangri-La

We climbed higher, into the clouds, into Tibet -- or at least into the Tibetan cultural world. Shangri-La sits at 3,160 meters on the edge of the Tibetan Plateau, and you feel every meter of that altitude in your lungs. The town was renamed from Zhongdian in 2001, borrowing the name from James Hilton's novel Lost Horizon. Bold move, but honestly? The place lives up to it.

Songzanlin Monastery -- the little Potala Palace

Songzanlin Monastery is the largest Tibetan Buddhist monastery in Yunnan, and approaching it feels like arriving at a fortress-city. Gold roofs glint in the high-altitude sun, and the white-and-red buildings cascade down the hillside in tiers. Inside, the air is thick with juniper incense and the low hum of monks chanting. I sat for a while and just listened.

Dukezong Old Town -- rebuilt and resilient

Dukezong Old Town was largely destroyed by fire in 2014 and has been carefully rebuilt. Walking its streets, you see the blend of old and new -- ancient stone foundations supporting fresh timber frames. The giant prayer wheel on the hilltop takes several people working together to turn, which I thought was a nice metaphor for something, though I have not figured out what yet.

"At 3,160 meters, take it easy on your first day. Drink lots of water, skip the baijiu, and do not be alarmed if you get a little headachy. It passes."

6

Yuanyang Rice Terraces

The grand finale: a landscape carved by centuries of patience. The Hani people have been sculpting these hillsides into terraced rice paddies for over 1,300 years, and the result is one of the most visually staggering things I have ever seen. Thousands of curved, flooded terraces cascade down the mountainsides, each one reflecting the sky like a shard of broken mirror.

Yuanyang -- 1,300 years of sculpting the earth

Sunrise is the magic hour here. We woke at 5 AM and drove through fog to a viewpoint overlooking the Duoyishu terraces. As the sun crept over the ridge, the terraces lit up in golds and pinks and silvers, the water catching the light in ways that made the whole hillside shimmer. Nobody spoke. We just watched.

The Hani villages themselves are worth exploring -- mushroom-shaped houses with thatched roofs, narrow stone paths, and a pace of life that makes Dali look like Manhattan. These terraces are a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and standing among them you understand exactly why. This is farming as art, patience as landscape architecture.

"The best time to visit Yuanyang is November through March, when the terraces are flooded but not yet planted. The water turns each terrace into a mirror for the sky."

Food Stamps Collection

No storybook is complete without snacks. Here are the edible souvenirs I collected along the way -- each one stamped into my memory forever.

Crossing-the-Bridge Noodles

Kunming's signature dish. A bubbling bowl of broth arrives with a parade of raw ingredients you add yourself -- thin rice noodles, sliced meat, quail eggs, chrysanthemum petals. The broth is sealed under a layer of oil to keep it volcanic.

Erkuai

Dali's rice cakes, grilled over charcoal until the outside is crispy and the inside is chewy. Slathered with chili paste and fermented bean sauce. Dangerously addictive street food.

Baba Flatbread

Lijiang's Naxi flatbread, stuffed with either savory ham and scallions or sweet rose petal jam. Cooked on a griddle until golden. Perfect walking-around food.

Yak Butter Tea

Shangri-La's fuel of choice. Salty, buttery, slightly funky tea churned with yak butter. An acquired taste that you will either love immediately or learn to love by your third cup.

Wild Mushroom Hotpot

Yunnan is the mushroom capital of China. A bubbling pot of broth loaded with porcini, matsutake, chanterelles, and varieties that do not have English names. Earthy, rich, unforgettable.

Pu'er Tea

Yunnan's famous fermented tea, aged in cakes for years or decades. The good stuff tastes like dark earth and dried fruit and autumn. Buy some at a tea house and watch the seller brew it with ceremonial precision.

Notes for the Road

Best time to visit: March through May, or September through November. Summer brings monsoon rains. Winter is chilly in the north but perfect for Yuanyang.
Getting around: Fly into Kunming, then use a mix of high-speed rail, buses, and hired drivers. The train from Kunming to Dali takes about two hours and is excellent.
Altitude: Shangri-La sits above 3,000 meters. Give yourself a day to acclimatize. Drink water. Walk slowly. Do not be a hero.
Language: Mandarin is widely spoken, but many locals also speak Bai, Naxi, Tibetan, or Hani. English is limited outside tourist hubs. A translation app is essential.
Money: Mobile payment (WeChat Pay, Alipay) is king. Some places do not accept cash at all. Set up a payment app before you arrive if you can.
Packing: Layers. Seriously. You can go from 25 degrees in Kunming to 5 degrees in Shangri-La in the same trip. Sun protection is critical at altitude -- the UV is no joke.
Photography: Sunrise at Yuanyang, sunset at Erhai Lake, and the Stone Forest at midday when the shadows are sharpest. Bring extra batteries -- cold altitude drains them fast.

And that is the end of this little storybook -- but the real story is the one you will write when you go yourself. Yunnan is waiting.
