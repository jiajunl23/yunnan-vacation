A Journey Through Yunnan

Episode One

In the far southwest of China, where the Himalayas begin their descent toward the tropics, there lies a province unlike any other. This is Yunnan. A land of twenty-five peoples. A land where the altitude shifts by four thousand metres in the space of a morning's drive.

To travel through Yunnan is to move through climates, through cultures, through time itself. From the subtropical lowlands where rice terraces catch the light of a thousand suns, to the frozen passes of the Tibetan plateau, this single province contains more biological and cultural diversity than most nations on Earth.

Chapter One

The Stone Forest

Shilin, eastern Yunnan

The limestone pinnacles of Shilin, sculpted by 270 million years of dissolution

Two hundred and seventy million years ago, this was the floor of a shallow sea. What stands here now defies simple description. Towers of limestone, some thirty metres tall, rise from the earth like the ruins of a petrified forest. The Sani people, a branch of the Yi, call it Shilin. The Stone Forest.

Walk among these pillars and you are walking through geological time made visible. Each groove, each overhang, each impossibly balanced blade of rock records an epoch of rainfall, of chemical patience. Water, the softest substance, has carved the hardest landscape in Yunnan.

The Sani have lived in the shadow of these stones for centuries. To them, the forest is not merely a geological curiosity. It is the setting of their most cherished legend -- the story of Ashima, a young woman of such beauty that she was turned to stone rather than be surrendered to a cruel landlord. Her profile, they say, can still be seen in the rock.

"To stand among these ancient pillars is to understand that the earth itself is an artist -- one whose medium is time, and whose patience is measured not in years, but in ages." -- Narrator

Chapter Two

Dali

The Bai heartland, western Yunnan

The Three Pagodas of Chongsheng Temple, standing since the 9th century

The road west from Kunming climbs steadily through eucalyptus groves and tobacco fields before dropping, quite suddenly, into a broad valley of extraordinary beauty. To the west, the Cang Mountains rise to over four thousand metres. To the east, Erhai Lake stretches forty kilometres into the haze. And between them sits the ancient city of Dali.

For five centuries, this was the capital of the Nanzhao and Dali kingdoms -- independent states that held their ground against the Tang and Song dynasties of China. The Three Pagodas, built during the reign of Nanzhao, still stand. They have survived earthquakes, invasions, and the slow erosion of a thousand years. They remain the most enduring symbol of a civilization that refuses to be forgotten.

The Bai people, who make up the majority here, are master builders and marble carvers. Their architecture is distinctive: white walls, gray tiles, intricate painted eaves. Walk through any Bai village and you will notice that every household faces east, toward the lake, and that every courtyard contains a screen wall -- designed, they say, to deflect evil spirits, who can only travel in straight lines.

Erhai Lake at dawn, with the Cang Mountains rising beyond

"The Bai have a saying: the mountains provide the stone, the lake provides the fish, and the wind provides the rest. In Dali, the wind is constant -- and so, it seems, is the sense of contentment." -- Narrator

Chapter Three

Lijiang

City of the Naxi, beneath the snow mountain

Lijiang Old Town, a labyrinth of canals and cobblestone lanes

Lijiang sits at 2,400 metres, in the shadow of a mountain the Naxi people call Yulong Xueshan -- the Jade Dragon Snow Mountain. The old town is a labyrinth. There are no city walls, no grid, no grand avenues. Instead, there are canals. Dozens of them, fed by the Black Dragon Pool, flowing through the town in channels that have been maintained for eight hundred years.

The Naxi are remarkable among China's minorities. They possess their own pictographic writing system -- Dongba script -- one of the last living hieroglyphic languages on Earth. Their priests, the Dongba, are keepers of a tradition that blends Tibetan Bon religion, Taoism, and indigenous animism into something entirely unique.

But it is the mountain that dominates everything. Jade Dragon Snow Mountain rises to 5,596 metres, its thirteen peaks permanently capped with snow and glacial ice. On clear mornings, the entire massif glows pink, then gold, then white. The Naxi say the mountain is a god. Standing before it at dawn, it is difficult to argue.

Jade Dragon Snow Mountain, 5,596 metres, perpetually crowned in ice

"The Naxi Dongba priests write in pictures. A sun is a circle with rays. A man is a figure walking. Death is a figure lying down. It is a language of beautiful, terrible simplicity -- and it has survived for over a thousand years." -- Narrator

Chapter Four

Tiger Leaping Gorge

Where the Yangtze cuts through the mountains

Tiger Leaping Gorge -- one of the deepest river canyons on Earth

North of Lijiang, the Jinsha River -- the upper Yangtze -- forces its way between Jade Dragon Snow Mountain and Haba Snow Mountain through a canyon so deep, so narrow, and so violent that it ranks among the most dramatic landscapes on the planet. Tiger Leaping Gorge. The name derives from a legend: a tiger, pursued by a hunter, is said to have leapt across the river at its narrowest point, using a midstream boulder as a stepping stone.

The gorge drops 3,900 metres from the mountain summits to the river below. The trail along its upper rim is considered one of the great walks of China -- two days of narrow paths cut into sheer cliff faces, with the roar of the river a constant companion far below.

This is the raw Yunnan. Not the carefully preserved old towns or the manicured tourist sites, but the province as geology made it: violent, immense, and humbling. The mountains here do not invite admiration. They demand respect.

"The river does not care for the mountain, and the mountain does not yield to the river. And yet, between them, they have created something neither could achieve alone -- a landscape of terrifying, indifferent beauty." -- Narrator

Chapter Five

Shangri-La

The Tibetan borderlands

Songzanlin Monastery, the largest Tibetan Buddhist monastery in Yunnan

The road north from Lijiang climbs relentlessly. The subtropical warmth falls away. Pine forests give way to alpine meadow, and then to high plateau grassland dotted with yak. At 3,200 metres, you arrive at a town that was, until 2001, called Zhongdian. That year, the local government renamed it Shangri-La -- after the fictional paradise in James Hilton's 1933 novel Lost Horizon. The marketing was audacious. But the landscape, it must be said, is persuasive.

Songzanlin Monastery dominates the hillside above the town. Built in 1679 on the orders of the Fifth Dalai Lama, it is often called the Little Potala Palace. Its golden roofs catch the thin highland sun. Inside, hundreds of monks maintain a tradition of Tibetan Buddhist scholarship that has endured, through upheaval and revolution, for over three centuries.

Below the monastery, the old town of Dukezong spreads across a low hill. Its name means "moonlight city" in Tibetan. The streets are worn smooth by centuries of foot traffic and yak caravans -- for this was once a critical stop on the Tea Horse Road, the ancient trade route that linked Yunnan's tea plantations with the Tibetan plateau.

Dukezong, the ancient "moonlight city" of the Tea Horse Road

"Whether or not this is the Shangri-La of Hilton's imagination is, in the end, beside the point. What matters is that the place exists at all -- a monastery on a hill, prayer flags in the wind, and a silence so deep you can hear the mountains breathe." -- Narrator

A Documentary Interlude

The Food of Yunnan

No account of Yunnan would be complete without its food. This is a province that eats differently from the rest of China. The cuisine is built on wild herbs, fermented vegetables, cured meats, and an astonishing variety of mushrooms -- many of which grow nowhere else on Earth. During the summer monsoon, the markets of Kunming overflow with porcini, matsutake, chanterelles, and dozens of species for which no English name exists.

Crossing the Bridge Noodles -- guoqiao mixian -- is the signature dish. A bowl of boiling broth arrives at the table sealed beneath a layer of hot oil, keeping the heat trapped within. The diner adds raw meat, quail eggs, vegetables, and rice noodles, which cook in seconds in the superheated liquid. It is a meal of ritual and precision, and its origin story involves a devoted wife, a scholar on an island, and a very long bridge.

In Dali, the Bai prepare erkuai -- thick rice cakes grilled over charcoal and wrapped around chili paste and pickled vegetables. In Shangri-La, yak butter tea is the staple -- salty, rich, and deeply warming at altitude. Each region, each people, each valley has its own table. To eat your way through Yunnan is to understand its diversity more intimately than any map could convey.

"In Yunnan, they say you can eat a different mushroom every day for a month and never repeat yourself. Some of those mushrooms, it should be noted, will make you see things. The Yunnanese consider this a feature, not a flaw." -- Narrator

Chapter Six

Yuanyang Rice Terraces

The carved mountains of the Hani people

The Yuanyang rice terraces, carved by the Hani over thirteen centuries

In the far south of Yunnan, near the Vietnamese border, the Ailao Mountains drop steeply toward the Red River valley. And on those slopes, the Hani people have, over the course of thirteen hundred years, carved one of the most extraordinary landscapes ever shaped by human hands. The Yuanyang rice terraces.

Thousands upon thousands of terraces cascade down the mountainside, following every contour, every fold of the terrain. In winter, when the paddies are flooded but not yet planted, they become mirrors -- reflecting the sky, the clouds, the setting sun. The effect, seen from the ridgeline above, is of a mountain made entirely of light.

The Hani irrigation system is a masterpiece of communal engineering. Water flows from the forested peaks through a network of channels that distributes it evenly across every terrace, every household, every field. No pump. No modern technology. Just gravity, cooperation, and the accumulated wisdom of fifty generations.

"The Hani have a proverb: the forest is the head, the village is the body, the terraces are the feet. Remove any part, and the whole falls. It is an understanding of ecology that Western science arrived at only in the last century. The Hani have practised it for a millennium." -- Narrator

Yunnan is not one place. It is many places, layered upon one another -- by altitude, by history, by the astonishing variety of peoples who call it home. To travel here is to be reminded that the world is larger, stranger, and more beautiful than we tend to assume. And that some of its greatest treasures are found not in the places everyone goes, but in the places where the road, quite simply, runs out.
