An Invitation to

Yunnan

A bespoke journey through China's most extraordinary province

The Experience

Yunnan is not merely a destination. It is a revelation -- a land where ancient cultures converge beneath snow-capped peaks, where terraced hillsides descend into subtropical valleys, and where every meal is a ceremony of flavor and tradition.

This curated itinerary unveils six unparalleled destinations, each selected for the discerning traveler who seeks authenticity wrapped in splendor.

Destination I

Kunming & the Stone Forest

The Spring City welcomes you with year-round temperate grace -- a fitting prelude to the grandeur ahead. Kunming's refined cadence sets the tone: lakeside promenades along Dianchi, unhurried afternoons in Green Lake Park, and evenings spent sampling Yunnan's celebrated culinary traditions.

Beyond the city, the Stone Forest rises from the earth in primordial splendor. These 270-million-year-old limestone karst formations -- towering, labyrinthine, otherworldly -- are a UNESCO World Heritage testament to nature's most extravagant artistry.

The Stone Forest -- Shilin, Yunnan

Destination II

Dali

Exclusive Experience

Dali is where Yunnan reveals its soul. Cradled between the Cangshan Mountains and the luminous expanse of Erhai Lake, this ancient seat of the Bai people offers a sensory richness that no itinerary can fully capture.

The Three Pagodas of Chongsheng Temple have stood sentinel for over a thousand years, their silhouettes mirrored in the reflecting pool below -- an image of timeless composure. Wander through Bai villages where tie-dye artisans practice techniques unchanged for centuries, and let the pace of life here recalibrate your own.

The Three Pagodas of Chongsheng Temple
Erhai Lake and the Cangshan Range

Destination III

Lijiang

Exclusive Experience

A UNESCO World Heritage town of uncommon beauty, Lijiang's Old Town is a living tapestry of Naxi heritage -- cobblestone lanes threaded with canals, centuries-old wooden courtyard homes, and the haunting strains of Naxi orchestral music drifting through evening air.

Rising beyond the old town, Jade Dragon Snow Mountain commands the horizon at 5,596 meters. Its glacial peaks, often veiled in cloud, are considered sacred by the Naxi people. A private ascent via cable car to the observation platform at 4,680 meters offers views of staggering, almost spiritual, magnitude.

Lijiang Old Town
Jade Dragon Snow Mountain

Destination IV

Tiger Leaping Gorge

For the traveler who craves visceral encounter with the sublime, Tiger Leaping Gorge delivers without reservation. One of the deepest river gorges on Earth, it carves a dramatic rift between Jade Dragon and Haba Snow Mountains as the Jinsha River thunders through its narrow passage.

The celebrated High Trail -- a two-day trek along the gorge rim -- is among the world's finest walks. Each turn reveals new theater: sheer cliff faces, roaring cataracts, and panoramas so vast they silence even the most seasoned adventurer.

Tiger Leaping Gorge

Destination V

Shangri-La

Exclusive Experience

The name alone conjures paradise -- and Shangri-La, perched at 3,300 meters on the Tibetan Plateau's edge, honors that promise. Here, the air is thin and luminous, the light unlike anywhere else on Earth.

Songzanlin Monastery, often called the "Little Potala Palace," rises in golden-roofed magnificence above the surrounding hills. Its 300-year-old halls house centuries of Tibetan Buddhist devotion. Nearby, the reconstructed Dukezong Old Town offers artisan workshops and traditional Tibetan tea houses, while the serene expanse of Napa Lake provides sanctuary for black-necked cranes and contemplative visitors alike.

Songzanlin Monastery
Dukezong Old Town, Shangri-La

Destination VI

Yuanyang Rice Terraces

The Hani people have sculpted these mountainsides for over 1,300 years, creating one of the most breathtaking agricultural landscapes ever conceived. Cascading from misty summits to valley floors, the terraces of Yuanyang are a UNESCO World Heritage masterwork of human ingenuity and ecological harmony.

Arrive at dawn. As the first light strikes the flooded paddies, the terraces ignite -- a thousand mirrored surfaces reflecting sky, cloud, and mountain in an ever-shifting mosaic of gold and silver. It is, without exaggeration, one of the most arresting sunrises on the planet.

Concierge Recommendations

The Culinary Journey

Yunnan's cuisine is among China's most distinctive -- a confluence of tropical abundance, highland austerity, and the culinary traditions of over two dozen ethnic groups. Our concierge has curated the essential tastings.

Signature Dishes

Crossing-the-Bridge Rice Noodles Kunming

The province's most celebrated dish. A rich bone broth served at a rolling boil, sealed beneath a layer of oil, into which raw ingredients are tableside-submerged -- an act of culinary theater and extraordinary flavor.

Steam Pot Chicken Jianshui

Prepared in a distinctive clay pot with a central chimney, the chicken steams in its own essence with goji berries and medicinal herbs. Pure, restorative, unforgettable.

Erkuai Kunming

Yunnan's signature rice cake -- grilled, pan-fried, or served in broth. Deceptively simple, profoundly satisfying. A staple of morning markets across the province.

Wild Mushroom Hot Pot Province-wide

Yunnan harbors the greatest diversity of edible fungi on Earth. During monsoon season, markets overflow with porcini, matsutake, and dozens of varieties found nowhere else. The communal hot pot is the definitive way to experience them.

Bai & Naxi Specialties

Bai Three-Course Tea Dali

A ceremonial tasting in three acts -- bitter, sweet, and lingering -- said to mirror life itself. Traditionally offered to honored guests. Accept with both hands.

Rushan (Milk Fan) Dali

A Bai dairy creation unlike anything in the Chinese culinary canon -- sheets of stretched goat cheese, grilled or fried, drizzled with rose syrup. A delicacy of arresting originality.

Naxi Baba Lijiang

A layered flatbread of the Naxi people, available in savory or sweet preparations. Best enjoyed fresh from the griddle in Lijiang's morning markets.

Libations

Pu'er Tea Xishuangbanna

Yunnan's gift to the world of tea. Aged cakes of fermented tea that improve over decades, commanding prices rivaling fine Bordeaux. Seek out ancient tree varieties for unparalleled depth.

Yunnan Coffee Pu'er Region

China's premier coffee-growing region produces arabica beans of surprising finesse. The specialty coffee scene in Kunming now rivals coastal cities. A revelation for the uninitiated.

Tibetan Butter Tea Shangri-La

Salted, buttered, and churned to a rich froth -- an acquired taste that becomes essential at altitude. Best taken in a Tibetan tea house with a view of the mountains.

Practical Information

Before You Depart

When to Visit

The ideal window is March through May and September through November. Spring brings wildflowers and mild temperatures; autumn offers crystalline skies and harvest-season color. Summer monsoons bring mushroom season but also rain. Winter is cold at altitude but rewards with solitude and snow-dusted monasteries.

Getting There

Kunming Changshui International Airport serves as the gateway, with direct flights from most major Asian cities. Internal flights connect to Lijiang and Shangri-La. The Kunming-Dali-Lijiang high-speed rail corridor offers a refined alternative, with first-class carriages providing panoramic views of the landscape.

Altitude Considerations

Shangri-La sits at 3,300 meters. Acclimatize for at least one full day before exertion. Ascend gradually -- Kunming (1,900m) to Dali (2,000m) to Lijiang (2,400m) to Shangri-La provides a natural progression. Carry supplemental oxygen for Jade Dragon Snow Mountain excursions.

Duration

A comprehensive journey through all six destinations requires a minimum of 14 days. For the unhurried traveler -- the only kind we recommend -- three weeks allows for serendipity, rest days, and the kind of lingering that transforms travel into genuine encounter.

Language

Mandarin is the lingua franca, though many ethnic communities speak their own languages. English is limited outside major tourist areas. A bespoke guide fluent in local languages and customs is not a luxury here -- it is a necessity.

Currency & Connectivity

China operates on the yuan (RMB). Mobile payment via WeChat Pay and Alipay is ubiquitous. International credit cards are accepted at upscale hotels. A local SIM card or eSIM with data is essential; VPN service should be arranged before arrival.
